1. Zone Activity
–

Analyzes all travel patterns in a zone set, regardless of origins or destinations

Zones:

  • Areas where trips start and end
  • Segments where trips pass through

Available Options:

  • Trip Attributes
  • Traveler Attributes
  • Inferred Home and Work Locations

Example:

  • Assessing hourly trends of trips occurring on Main St.
  • Assessing trips that end at San Francisco airport

2. Origin-Destination
–

Used to analyze trips that have an origin in one or more zone sets and a destination in one or more zone sets

Zones:

  • Areas where trips start and end
  • Segments where trips pass through

Available Options:

  • Trip Attributes
  • Traveler Attributes

Examples:

  • Assessing trips from a neighborhood to a mall
  • Assessing trips from the airport to different off ramps as destinations

3. Origin-Destination Through Middle Filters
–

Used to analyze trips that start in one or more zone sets, pass through one or more zone sets, and then end in one or more zone sets (often called Select Link in the industry)

Zones:

  • Origins and destinations are areas where trips start and end or segments where trips pass through
  • Middle filter is always a line segment where trips pass through

Available Options:

  • Trip Attributes
  • Traveler Attributes

Example:

  • Assessing the proportions of trips using each of three bridges that connect a city on the right side of the river to the left side
  • Assessing which freeway off ramps were used by trips originating in one neighborhood and ending in another neighborhood

4. Trips to or From Pre-Set Geography
–

Used to analyze trips that start or end in one or more zone sets and end or start in another preset group of geographies (ZIP codes, TAZs, etc.)

Zones

  • One zone set as origin or destination
  • Pre-set geographies
    • US Census Block Groups
    • US ZIP codes
    • US Traffic Analysis Zones
    • Canadian Dissemination Areas

Available Options

  • Trip Attributes
  • Traveler Attributes

Example:

  • Assessing which ZIP codes travelers are originating from when they arrive at the local stadium

5. Top Routes for Zones
–

Allows you to visualize road segments with the most traffic to, from, or through a selected zone

Zones:

  • Areas where trips start and end
  • Segments where trips pass through

Available Options:

  • Route segment types

Example:

  • Assessing which roads are most commonly used to arrive at the Central Business District of a city

6. Top Routes Between Origins and Destinations
–

Allows you to visualize road segments with the most traffic to, from, or through specified origin and destination zone sets

Zones:

  • Areas where trips start and end
  • Segments where trips pass through

Available Options:

  • Route segment types

Example:

  • Assessing the most commonly used roadways travelers use to get from the airport to the city center

7. AADT
–

Provides annual average daily traffic on specified roadways for the year chosen

Zones:

  • Segments where trips pass through

Available Options:

  • None

Example:

  • Getting AADT estimates for federal reporting requirements on state roads

8. Segment Analysis
–

Provides various Metrics that describe trips through corridors and road performance Metrics:

  1. Speed of trips
  1. Duration of trips
  1. "Free Flow Factor", which measures congestion
  1. Speed Percentile
  1. Travel Time Percentile
  1. Congestion
  1. Vehicle Miles of Travel/Vehicle Kilometers of Travel (VMT/VKT)
  1. Vehicle Hours of Delay (VHD)

Zones:

  • Segments where trips pass through

Available Options:

  • Speed
  • Duration
  • Speed Percentile
  • Travel Time Percentile

Example:

  • Assessing the proportion of vehicles traveling at free flow speed on a roadway

9. Turning Movement Counts
–

Provides Metrics for trips at intersections including:

  1. Counts of trips that turn left, right and continue straight
  1. Peak Hour Distribution
  1. Peak Hour Factor

Zones:

  • Gates for every leg of an intersection

Available Options:

  • None

Example:

  • Assessing the proportion of vehicles heading Eastbound that make left turns at an intersection

Visualization tool highlights critical insights in a simple layout that is easily shared and can be downloaded.

Metrics include:

12. Network Performance

Quickly calculate volume, speed, travel time and road performance metrics such as congestion and VMT.

  • Calculating the average time and congestion rate for trips from the start to the end of a line zone

StreetLight Volume

What is it?

  • An estimated number of vehicles
  • This is the only output that can be interpreted at face value as an actual volume of vehicles

Available for the following Modes of Travel:

  • All Vehicles LBS+ & All Vehicles CVD+
  • Trucks if using Zone Activity (Pass-Through Zones only) or Segment Analysis Type
  • Bicycle
  • Pedestrian

StreetLight Index

What is it?

  • A relative measure of activity of zones in space and time meaning it can be used to get ratios and percentage differences of trips between zones
  • Needs calibration to be converted to an estimate of trip numbers

Available for the following Modes of Travel:

  • Available for all modes except Bicycle and Pedestrian
    • StreetLight Index for different modes are not directly comparable as they are normalized using different methods (for example Rail Index versus Bicycle Index)

The Stark County Regional Planning Commission (SCRPC) contracted a transportation study assessing the viability of infrastructure upgrades to handle an expected growth in tourism to the Football Hall of Fame. Part of the assessment purpose was to find out where people were visiting from.

StreetLight All Vehicles CVD+ Methodology

Connected Vehicle Data has been integrated into new vehicles for almost a decade and in some cases longer. This technology is being or will be integrated into all new vehicles. Because we get anonymized data we cannot and will not know the precise demographics of vehicle owners.

  • Data Sources

    CVD+

    CVD+ (Connected Vehicle Data) incorporates data from personal vehicles with embedded location technology. CVD does not require mode classification because we know the data is collected from personal vehicles.

    The CVD+ data includes weather and road attributes.

  • Validation

    Ground-Truth Data

    The models are trained and validated with permanent counters across The United States (U.S). The CVD+ data utilized 2,785 permanent counters in 22 states.

  • Metrics

    Output in Viz3D & Downloads

    The data collected from Connected Vehicles goes through the Route Science® Processing Engine and is validated against permanent counters, the output is the StreetLight Insight® Metrics.

Differences between CVD+ and LBS+

Location-based Service (LBS+)

  • Data collected from smartphone apps that use opt-in location-based services
  • Has a vehicular bias, pings move like a motorized vehicle
  • 1-2 minute ping rate
  • Output Types: Volume, Index & Single Factor Calibrated Index
  • More suitable if you are interested enhanced data e.g., socio-demographics or Home and Work Location (HWL)

Connected Vehicle Data (CVD+)

  • OEM enabled connected data from “smart” vehicles
  • Known travel mode
  • 3 second ping rate
  • Output Types: All Vehicles Volume
  • Currently no enhanced data e.g., socio-demographics or Home and Work Location (HWL)
  • Increased accuracy of core network speed, trip length, travel time and trip circuity results

  1. Traffic Congestion

    Traffic congestion can be defined as the percentage reduction in speed from the Free Flow Speed. StreetLight defines congestion as 1 - Free Flow Factor where the Free Flow Factor is the average Trip Speed divided by the Free Flow Speed

  1. Free Flow Travel Time

    Free Flow Travel Time is the time required to travel a roadway section under low-volume conditions. It can be considered the “ideal” travel time on a roadway.

  1. Percentile Travel Time

    Percentile Travel Time is a measure of travel duration on a road segment, indicating the time met or exceeded by a certain percentage of trips. For example, the 50th Percentile Travel Time (median) is the time that half of all trips take or less.

  1. Planning Time & Planning Time Index

    Planning Time is defined as the 95th Percentile Travel Time. It is the time required for completing trips ontime 95% of the time.

    Planning Time Index (PTI) normalizes the planning time by dividing it by the “ideal” or Free-Flow Travel Time.

  1. Level of Travel Time Reliability (LOTTR)

    The LOTTR is defined as the 80th-Percentile Travel Time divided by the 50th-Percentile Travel Time. The greater the LOTTR value, the longer travel times are on relatively poor (but not uncommon) travel days, compared to travel times on typical days.

    FHWA considers LOTTR < 1.5 as reliable

  1. Travel Time Index

    Travel Time Index is a measure of how long it takes to travel under the selected day types and day parts compared to the travel time at free-flow speeds.

  1. Unreliable Segments

    Unreliable Segments are those that have a Level of Travel Time Reliability that is greater than 1.5 or 150%.

  1. Reliability Factor

    The percent of miles that are considered reliable or having consistent travel times with low variability

Vehicles Miles Traveled

VMT of roadways is a measure of a roadway’s use relative to a region or other roads.

It is a primary metric for regional travel analysis.

It enables a normalization metric for

  • Travel Time Reliability Metrics
  • Average traffic over a defined segment of corridor
  • Vehicle Hours of Delay
  • Crash Rate Analysis
  • Emissions studies - both point and regional and point-source

VMT = Volume * Road Length

Vehicle Hours of Delay

  • Used to analyze congestion in an area
  • Integral for transportation planning and policy decisions.
  • Defined as an aggregate metric that represents the amount of time vehicles spend in congestion versus the time travel would take in free flow conditions.

Using StreetLight InSight®, we can calculate VHD in a Segment Analysis

VHD can be combined with a Vehicle Occupancy Factor to derive total Person Hours of Delay.

VHD for Day Part = (VMT/Average segment speed) - (VMT/Segment free flow speed)

Peak Hour Factor

A Metric that indicates the consistency of traffic volume during the peak traffic hour. In StreetLight InSight®, you can get it by running a Turning Movement Counts Analysis.

Peak Hour Factor (PHF) metrics compare the traffic volume during the busiest 15-minutes of the peak hour with the total volume during the peak hour.

PHF is between 0 and 1, where a PHF closer to 1 indicates that the traffic flow is consistent throughout the hour. This number can then be used in roadway capacity planning.

Match the Reliability Metric to the correct equation

  • Vehicle Miles Traveled = Volume * Road Length
  • Planning Time Index = (95th Percentile Travel Time) / (Free Flow Travel Time)
  • Congestion =1 - Free Flow Factor

Trip Purpose

Steps to Tackle a General Mobility Question

Once you have a broad idea of your mobility question, follow these steps:

  • 1

    Start with an exploratory analysis over the area by using zones in the Zone Library.

    Try the following analyses:

    Top Routes or Top Routes OD for results on routes used

    Trips to or from Pre-set Geography for results on trips starting or ending at standard       geographies like US Census Tracts, Zip Codes, Block Groups, or Canadian                           Dissemination Areas

    Zone Activity with Home and Work Locations (HWL) for results on where travelers in       your zone live and work

  • 2

    Run analyses to focus on the Metrics you need based on the results from step one.   

    Define your transportation question more specifically with geographic and temporal         aspects   

    Use analyses that specify origin, destination, and middle filter (e.g. OD, ODMF, TMC,           Segment Analysis)

  • 3

    Rerun your analyses with slightly modified configurations for comparison.   

    Analyze mobility patterns over time, by different modes, or with additional trip or               traveler attributes   

    Change day types and day parts to increase the granularity of your Metrics

National Performance Management Measures

Travel time reliability measures are included in several federal reporting requirements like Moving Ahead for Progress in the 21st Century (MAP-21) which establishes a system reliability goal.

Transportation Performance Management (TPM) Performance Measure Rule 3 (PM3) includes the following measures:

  • Interstate Travel Time Reliability Measure - using Level Of Travel Time Reliability (LOTTR)
  • Non-interstate Travel Time Reliability Measure – using Level Of Travel Time Reliability (LOTTR)
  • Freight Reliability Measure – using Truck Travel Time Reliability (TTTR)
  • Peak Hour Excessive Delay – using vehicle hours of delay (VHD)

As of January 2022, federal reporting requirements related to PM3 must be fulfilled using data from the National Performance Measurement Research Data Set (NPMRDS).

For local, state, and MPO planning, StreetLight can provide a wealth of Metrics that offer insight on travel time reliability.

Value of Reliable Travel Times

  • Improvements targeted at TTR reduce delay due to traffic-influencing events
  • Addressing the major components of unreliable travel (crashes, bad weather, and work zones) leads to safer highways
  • More reliable travel times save time, fuel, and money for commuters and freight carriers

Examples of Reliability Metrics:

  • Percentile travel times (50th, 80th, 90th, 95th)
  • Planning time and planning time index
  • Buffer time and buffer time index
  • Level Of Travel Time Reliability (LOTTR)
  • Truck Travel Time Reliability (TTTR)

Buffer time represents the extra amount of time a traveler would need to budget for a trip to ensure an on time arrival 95% of the time. It is calculated as the 95th percentile travel minus the average travel time.

Buffer index is size of the buffer as a percentage of the average (95th percentile minus the average , divided by the average)

Buffer Index = (TT95 - TTAverage) / TTAverage * 100

The TTTR Index is defined as the 95th percentile truck travel time divided by the 50th percentile truck travel time.

How to Use LOTTR for TTR Reporting

TPM’s interstate and non-interstate travel time reliability measures combine LOTTR at different day types and parts to calculate a percent person-miles traveled that are reliable. For each directional interstate roadway section, LOTTR is calculated for each of the following four reliability reporting periods:

  • 1

    All weekday am peak periods (6am to 10am) during a calendar year [AMP]

  • 2

    All weekday midday periods (10am to 4pm) during a calendar year [MIDD]

  • 3

    All weekday p.m. peak periods (4pm to 8pm) during a calendar year [PMP]

  • 4

    All weekend daytime periods (Saturday and Sunday, 6am to 8pm) during a calendar year [WE]

Interstate travel time reliability is then calculated as the length – and person trip – weighted percentage of interstate roadway sections that have LOTTRs less than 1.50 during all four periods.

How to Calculate TTTR Index

For each roadway section, a TTTR Index is calculated for each of the following five reliability reporting periods:

  • 1

    All weekday am peak periods (6am to 10am) during a calendar year [AMP]

  • 2

    All weekday midday periods (10am to 4pm) during a calendar year [MIDD]

  • 3

    All weekday p.m. peak periods (4pm to 8pm) during a calendar year [PMP]

  • 4

    All weekend daytime periods (Saturday and Sunday, 6am to 8pm) during a calendar year [WE]

  • 5

    All nighttime periods (Sunday through Saturday, 8pm to 6am) during a calendar year [OVN]

FHWA’s freight reliability measure is calculated as the length-weighted average of the maximum of the five TTTR Index values for each Interstate roadway segment in the state.

Vehicle miles / kilometers traveled (VMT / VKT)
–

  • VMT of roadways is a measure of a roadway’s use relative to a region or other roads
  • It is a primary metric for regional travel analysis
  • It enables a normalization metric for:
    • Travel time reliability metrics
    • Average traffic over a defined segment of corridor
    • Vehicle hours of delay
    • Crash rate analysis
    • Emissions studies (both point and regional and point-source)

VMT = Volume * Road Length

Vehicle hours of delay
–

  • Used to analyze congestion in an area
  • Integral for transportation planning and policy decisions
  • Defined as an aggregate metric that represents the amount of time vehicles spend in congestion versus the time it would take to travel in free flow conditions
